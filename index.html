Q1Sodium Loading

A 72-year-old male with moderate renal impairment (eGFR 38 mL/min) is admitted to the ICU with a complicated urinary tract infection caused by a carbapenem-resistant organism. He is started on high-dose IV antibiotic therapy dosed in grams per day. On day 3, his serum sodium rises to 158 mEq/L. Which antibiotic is most likely responsible for this sodium overload?

The Insight
Fosfomycin is formulated as disodium salt for IV use. Each gram contains approximately 14 mmol (320 mg) of sodium. At typical doses of 16 g/day for serious infections, this delivers over 230 mEq of sodium daily — equivalent to less than 500 mL of 3% saline. This makes fosfomycin the antibiotic most notorious for causing iatrogenic hypernatremia, especially in patients with renal impairment or oliguria who cannot excrete the excess sodium load efficiently.
